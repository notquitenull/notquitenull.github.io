Correlating Donald Trump's Tweets with Polymarket Price Movement

This project was developed as part of the course "Computational Methods for Open Source Intelligence" at Ruhr University Bochum. It explores the potential impact of Donald Trump's social media posts on prediction markets using publicly available data.

In particular, we combined social media data with historical Polymarket data to investigate whether Donald Trump's public statemets are associated with changes in prediction market prices.

Problem Formulation

Polymarket is one of the most profitable prediction market in the world. A prediction market allows people to bet on real world events, for financial gains. Polymarket is comprised of markets. Each market contains a question or statements and two or more answers to bet money on.
Donald Trump, the 45. and 47 President of the United States of America, is known for his activity on Twitter and later on Truth Social. He often posts about real world events.
We think it is particularly interesting whether these posts have any correlation with betting activity on Polymarket.

Ethics

For ethics, we considered two main aspects:
  • We use and scrape publicly available posts from Donald Trump. Since Donald Trump is a public figure and these posts represent his public statements, we consider their use to raise no significant privacy concerns.
  • The Polymarket dataset we are using contains userdata, in the form of usernames and ids. However, our analysis does not use any user data and focuses only on market data. Furthermore, the available information does not allow us to identify individual users.

Data Collection

We downloaded historical Polymarket data from the following publicly available dataset: Polymarket dataset on Hugging Face .

  • 107GB dataset containing all historical data until early 2026

Limitations

This analysis has several limitations.

First, we cannot determine whether a market change is directly caused by a specific post or whether both the post and the market movement are responses to the same external event.

Furthermore, our use of broad topic labels can lead to mismatches between social media posts and prediction markets, as posts and markets may be classified as related despite referring to different aspects of a topic.

Finally, our analysis focuses only on changes in market prices from a yes-no perspective and does not take betting volume or trading activity into account, which could provide additional insights into the significance of market movements.
